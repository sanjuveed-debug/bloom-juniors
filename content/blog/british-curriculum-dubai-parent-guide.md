If you've just enrolled your child in a British-curriculum school in Dubai, you've probably had this experience: the school sends a warm welcome email containing the sentence *"Your child will join FS2, working within the EYFS framework towards the ELGs."*

Every parent smiles and nods. Almost nobody knows what it means — including, a few years ago, me.

Here's the full decoder: what each stage is, what your child actually learns in it, and what (if anything) you should be doing at home.

## First, the year groups — and the age confusion

British schools in the UAE use British year names, which don't match American grades or the ages you might expect:

| Stage | Age | What it's called elsewhere | Framework |
|-------|-----|---------------------------|-----------|
| **FS1** (Foundation Stage 1) | 3–4 | Nursery / Pre-K | EYFS |
| **FS2** (Foundation Stage 2) | 4–5 | Reception / Kindergarten | EYFS |
| **Year 1** | 5–6 | 1st Grade | National Curriculum (KS1) |
| **Year 2** | 6–7 | 2nd Grade | National Curriculum (KS1) |

Two acronyms to decode:

- **EYFS** — Early Years Foundation Stage, the UK framework covering ages 0–5. It governs FS1 and FS2 and is built around learning through *play*, not desks and worksheets.
- **KS1** — Key Stage 1, the first stage of the "proper" National Curriculum, covering Years 1 and 2.

The UK equivalent of FS1/FS2 is "Nursery and Reception" — useful to know when you're reading British parenting resources, which rarely use the FS labels.

## What your child actually learns in FS1 (age 3–4)

FS1 looks like playing. That's not a bug — the EYFS is explicit that children this age learn *through* play. Under the surface, teachers are working on seven areas of learning, and the ones parents ask about most are:

- **Communication and language** — listening, following instructions, building vocabulary
- **Early literacy** — this is **Phase 1 phonics**: rhymes, clapping syllables, spotting sounds in words. *No letters yet* — it's all listening. Plus early mark-making (the scribbles that become writing)
- **Early maths** — counting to 5 and beyond by touching objects one at a time, recognising shapes, comparing big/small and more/fewer
- **Personal, social and emotional development** — sharing, turn-taking, independence (this one quietly matters more than all the others at this age)

**What to do at home:** read to them daily, sing rhymes, count real things (stairs, grapes, toy cars), and talk — endlessly. That's genuinely the whole list.

## What your child actually learns in FS2 (age 4–5)

FS2 (Reception) is the biggest learning year of your child's life. The headline events:

- **Phonics begins in earnest** — Phase 2 (the first 19 letter sounds) in the first term, Phase 3 (25 more, including digraphs like *sh* and *ch*) after that. By the end of FS2, most children can blend simple words and read short decodable sentences. We've written a full [parent's guide to Phase 2 phonics](/blog/phase-2-phonics-sounds-explained) if your child is at this stage.
- **Reading and writing** — recognising "tricky words" (the, said, was), writing their name, forming letters, and writing simple words by sounding them out
- **Maths** — counting reliably to 20, understanding that the last number counted *is* the amount (this is called cardinality, and it's a bigger cognitive leap than it sounds), simple addition and subtraction with objects, patterns and shapes
- **The ELGs** — Early Learning Goals, the checkpoints teachers assess at the end of FS2. Your end-of-year report will say whether your child is "emerging" or "expected" against each goal. One year of the word "emerging" is not a crisis — children this age develop in bursts.

**What to do at home:** 10 minutes a day. Practise the sounds the school is teaching that week (ask the teacher — they'll happily tell you), count everything, and read together every night. Consistency beats intensity by a mile.

## What changes in Year 1 and Year 2 (KS1)

Year 1 is a real gear-change: the play-based EYFS gives way to the National Curriculum with defined subjects.

- **Year 1:** phonics continues (Phases 4–5, where spellings get more complex), children move from reading words to reading books, maths goes to 100 with number bonds and real addition/subtraction. In June, all children take the **Phonics Screening Check** — a short, low-key check where they read 40 words (some invented, like "vap") to a teacher. Schools prepare children well; parents mostly just need to keep reading at home.
- **Year 2:** reading for *comprehension* (not just decoding), writing sentences with joined ideas, times tables begin (2s, 5s, 10s), and money, measurement and simple fractions arrive in maths.

**What to do at home:** listen to them read aloud daily — it's the single highest-value activity for this age band. For maths, make it real: paying with cash at the supermarket, halving a pizza, telling the time.

## The Dubai-specific parts

A few things that apply here and not in the UK:

- **KHDA ratings** — Dubai's school regulator inspects every private school annually and publishes ratings from *Outstanding* to *Weak*. Worth reading your school's latest report — but read the early-years section specifically, as a school can be strong in secondary and average in FS.
- **September birthdays and cut-offs** — UAE British schools use the UK academic year (September to August). A child born in August will be the youngest in the class; one born in September, the oldest. Nearly a full year of development separates them — keep that in mind before comparing your child to classmates.
- **Arabic** — Arabic language study is mandatory in UAE schools (from Year 2 for non-native speakers, earlier in many schools). It's a KHDA requirement, not a school choice.

## The honest summary

If you remember three things from this whole guide:

1. **FS1 = learning through play. Don't rush it.** The child who can share and listen will learn to read faster than the one who was drilled at 3.
2. **FS2 = phonics is the main event.** Ten minutes of daily practice at home, using pure sounds, is the highest-impact thing any parent can do.
3. **Little and often, always.** At every stage from 3 to 7, short daily practice beats weekend marathons.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is FS1 compulsory in Dubai?**
No — compulsory schooling starts later. But most British schools expect children to join by FS2, and places in good schools often fill from FS1.

**What's the difference between FS2 and Reception?**
Nothing — same year, different name. UAE schools say FS2; UK schools say Reception.

**My child is in FS2 and can't read yet. Is that normal?**
In the first half of the year, completely. By the end of FS2, most children blend simple words — but the range of "normal" is wide. Talk to the teacher before worrying; ask which specific phonics phase your child is working in.

**Do British-curriculum children in Dubai take UK exams?**
Eventually yes — GCSEs and A-Levels in secondary school. In FS and KS1 the only national check is the Year 1 Phonics Screening Check.
