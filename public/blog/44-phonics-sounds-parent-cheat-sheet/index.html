The 44 Phonics Sounds: A Parent's Cheat Sheet (With Example Words)

English has 26 letters — but 44 sounds. That single fact explains almost everything confusing about how your child is being taught to read: why sh is treated as one thing, why igh gets its own flashcard, and why the teacher keeps saying "sounds, not letters."

This is the complete list of all 44, in plain parent language, with example words for each. Bookmark it — you'll be back during every phonics phase.

Two words worth knowing (then no more jargon)

  • A phoneme is a sound — the smallest sound in a word. The word cat has three: c–a–t.
  • A grapheme is how a sound is written — one letter (s), two (sh), or three (igh).

That's the whole trick of English: 44 sounds sharing 26 letters, so some sounds get letter teams. When your child's school says "digraph," they mean a two-letter team; "trigraph" means three.

One more essential: say every sound pure — "sss" not "suh," "mmm" not "muh." Adding "uh" is the number-one habit that breaks blending. (We've written a full guide to Phase 2 sounds and pure pronunciation.)

When does my child learn each one?

Rough map to the phonics phases most British-curriculum schools use:

  • Phase 2 (first term of Reception/FS2): 19 sounds — s, a, t, p, i, n, m, d, g, o, c, k, ck, e, u, r, h, b, f, l (with ff, ll, ss spellings)
  • Phase 3 (rest of Reception): the remaining 25 — j, v, w, x, y, z, zz, qu, plus all the digraphs (ch, sh, th, ng) and vowel sounds (ai, ee, igh, oa, oo, ar, or, ur, ow, oi, ear, air, ure, er)
  • Phases 4–5 (Year 1): no new sounds — instead, new spellings of sounds they already know (ay for ai, ea for ee…), which is why Year 1 feels like phonics all over again

So by the end of Reception, your child has met all 44 sounds at least once. Everything after that is spelling variety and fluency.

How to use this list at home

  1. Match the school, don't run ahead. Ask the teacher which sounds are current this week and practise those. Five focused minutes beats an hour of everything.
  2. Sounds first, letter names later. The alphabet song can wait; reading is built on "sss," not "ess."
  3. Play, don't drill. "I spy something beginning with sh" in the car does more than a worksheet.
  4. Little and often. Ten minutes a day, every day. This is the entire secret.

Frequently asked questions

Why 44? I've seen 42 or 46 in other lists. Linguists genuinely disagree at the edges (whether ure counts, how to treat the two th sounds). 44 is the count used by Letters and Sounds and most UK school schemes — it's the number your child's teacher means.

Does my child need to know the terms phoneme and digraph? No. Teachers use them; children just need the sounds. You don't need them either — "sound" and "letter team" work fine at home.

What are the hardest sounds for children? The vowel digraphs cause the most confusion (oo in book vs moon), plus r/w mixing in speech. Both are developmental and usually resolve with practice and time.

My child speaks another language at home. Does this list change? The 44 sounds are English-specific — some won't exist in your home language and take longer to hear (Arabic speakers often find p/b tricky; that's completely normal). Bilingual children catch up fast; keep the home language strong, it helps rather than hurts.
